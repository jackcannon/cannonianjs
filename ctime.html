Why?

Why should you use Cannonian Time? Why shouldn't you?
Cannonian Time is a metric-style time measurement which is fully compatible with the Gregorian Calender. 'Invented' by Jack Cannon, it replaces 24 hours in a day with 100 hours in a day (1 Cannonian Hour is equal to 14 minutes, 23 seconds under traditional methods). Each of these hours are split into 10 Cannonian minutes, which, in turn, is divided into 100 centi-minutes.

But WHY?

Have you ever tried to do complex mathematics with time? It gets mighty complicated with the 24/60/60 time format, right? As humans, our mathematical education revolves around base-10 (decimal/denery) because our number system is based upon what our ancestors could count on their fingers. We've adopted base-10 units of measurements for many different things, including length (metres), volume (litres) and even temperature (celsius & kelvin), but our society insists on continuing with an inferior measuring system for time. Alas, dates are based upon rotations (days), lunar cycles (months) and orbits (years), and as we cant control these elements, the Gregorian calender is adequate (whilst certainly not perfect).But why do we have 24 hours? Because our elders liked the number 12. There are 12 zodiac signs, 12 days of Christmas, 12 deciples of Jesus and 12 olympian Gods. 12 is a number easily fractioned into halves or thirds, and therefore, in times passed, it made mathematical sense to base our measurements on it. However, in the modern age, our priorities for division are beyond halves and thirds. We need a time system based upon the decimal system, even if just for calculations. That's where Cannonian time comes in. Mathmeticians now use base-10 lengths or degrees (radians), scientists now use base-10 volumes, so why should programmers calculate using flawed systems? Cannonian time is a system perfectly designed for precise or general time-based calculation. Convert to Cannonian, do your calculations, and then, if needed, convert back to 'traditional time'!
